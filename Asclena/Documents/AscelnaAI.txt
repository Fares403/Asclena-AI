1.1 The Motivation: Beyond the Black Box
The deployment of Artificial Intelligence in healthcare faces a critical dilemma known
as the ”Black Box Problem.” Traditional Deep Learning models, particularly Convolu-
tional Neural Networks (CNNs), excel at pattern recognition but lack interpretability.
Conversely, modern Generative AI models (LLMs/VLMs) offer excellent communication
skills but suffer from ”Hallucinations”—generating confident but factually incorrect med-
ical advice.
Asclena addresses this by rejecting the idea of a single monolithic model. Instead,
it adopts a Epistemic Reasoning and Methodological Skepticism (a consultation team),
separating the cognitive processes into three distinct epistemic roles:
1.Perception: Quantitative analysis of visual data (CNNs).
2.Interpretation: Semantic understanding and reporting (VLMs).
3.Verification: Fact-checking against established medical literature (Agentic RAG).

This chapter provides a comprehensive theoretical background on the application of Deep
Learning in the medical domain. It explores the mathematical foundations of Convolu-
tional Neural Networks (CNNs) and the evolution of architectures suitable for diagnostic
imaging.
2.1 Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
2.1.1 Core Concept
A Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a specialized neural architecture designed to
process data with a known grid-like topology. In medical imaging, this data can be 2D
(e.g., X-Rays, Histopathology slides) or 3D volumetric data (e.g., CT scans, MRI).
Unlike fully connected networks that treat inputs as a flat vector, CNNs operate on
the premise of ”Local Connectivity” and”Parameter Sharing.”
Medical Importance (Translation Invariance): In medical diagnostics, a lesion
(e.g., a tumor or nodule) can appear anywhere within an organ. The translation invariance
property of CNNs ensures that the network recognizes the pathological feature regardless
of its spatial location (e.g., whether the tumor is in the upper or lower lobe of the lung).
This eliminates the need for manual alignment of images.
2.1.2 The Anatomy of a CNN
A standard CNN is composed of a sequence of layers that transform the input volume into
an output class or map. Each layer plays a critical role in extracting clinically relevant
information.
1. Convolutional Layer (The Feature Extractor)
The core building block. A set of learnable filters (kernels) slides over the input image.
Mathematical Operation: It performs a dot product between the filter entries
and the input, producing a ”Feature Map.”
2
Clinical Significance (Hierarchical Learning):
–Early Layers: Act as ”Edge Detectors,” identifying anatomical boundaries such
as the rib cage, heart borders, or diaphragm.
–Deep Layers: Capture abstract ”Textural Features.” In conditions like Pneu-
monia, the disease manifests not as a shape but as a ”cloudy” texture (Opaci-
ties). The deep convolutional layers are capable of distinguishing these subtle
texture changes from healthy tissue.
2. Activation Functions (Non-Linearity)
Linear operations alone cannot model complex biological data. Activation functions in-
troduce non-linearity.
ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit): f(x) =max(0, x).
Medical Advantage: Biological structures are highly non-linear and complex.
ReLU allows the network to learn intricate decision boundaries between healthy
and diseased tissues efficiently, accelerating the training process without suffering
from the vanishing gradient problem in early stages.
3. Pooling Layers (Downsampling)
Function: Reduces the spatial dimensions (Resolution) to decrease computational
cost and control overfitting.
Types: Max Pooling (takes the strongest signal) and Average Pooling (smooths
the signal).
Medical Advantage: Max Pooling acts as a feature selector. For example, if a
small but high-intensity calcification (micro-calcification) exists in a mammogram,
Max Pooling preserves this critical high-value signal while discarding less relevant
background noise.

2.2 Evolution of Architectures in Medicine
The choice of architecture dictates the model’s ability to learn medical features. The field
has evolved significantly to address the challenges of depth and feature preservation.
2.2.1 1. ResNet (Residual Networks)
As networks became deeper to capture more complex pathologies, training became impos-
sible due to the ”Vanishing Gradient” problem. ResNet introduced ”Skip Connections”
(or Identity Shortcuts).
Concept: It allows the gradient to bypass layers, flowing directly backward during
backpropagation.
Medical Use Case: ResNet is the standard backbone for detecting complex dis-
eases that require very deep semantic understanding, such as differentiating between
various types of malignant skin lesions (Melanoma vs. Nevus).
2.2.2 2. DenseNet (Densely Connected Networks)
DenseNet took the skip connection idea further. Instead of adding inputs (like ResNet),
itconcatenates them.
Feature Reuse: Every layer receives inputs from all previous layers.
Why it is Critical for X-Rays: In medical imaging, ”Low-level” features (edges/-
textures) are often as important as ”High-level” features.
Clinical Advantage: DenseNet preserves fine-grained details throughout the net-
work. This makes it superior for detecting pathologies like Infiltration orNodules ,
where the disease manifests as a subtle local variation in tissue density rather than
a gross shape change. The ”collective knowledge” of all layers is available for the
final diagnosis.

While previous models focused on scaling one dimension (usually depth), EfficientNet

The Concept of Compound Scaling
EfficientNet demonstrates that balancing network depth, width, and image resolution
leads to better performance than scaling any single dimension alone.

Medical Advantage (Efficiency at Scale): In a real-world hospital environment,

rapidly without requiring massive computational infrastructure. It achieves state-of-the-
art accuracy with significantly fewer parameters (FLOPS), ensuring that the ”Perception
Layer” of the system remains responsive even under high load.

2.3 Computer Vision Tasks in Medicine
Deep Learning is not just about classification. In the medical domain, we perform several
distinct tasks, each solving a specific clinical problem:
2.3.1 1. Classification (Diagnosis)
Assigning a diagnostic label to an image.
Binary: Normal vs. Abnormal.
Multi-Label: Identifying multiple co-existing conditions (e.g., Cardiomegaly +
Effusion), which is common in clinical practice.
Role: Triage tool to prioritize sick patients in a work-list.

The process of partitioning an image into meaningful segments.
Role: Critical for radiotherapy planning (outlining the tumor volume) and quanti-

Architecture: U-Net is the standard for this task.
2.3.3 3. Detection (Localization)
Identifying the location of a lesion using a Bounding Box.
Role: Acts as a ”Second Reader” for radiologists, highlighting suspicious regions

2.4 Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in Medical Imag-
ing
2.4.1 Overview & Motivation
While Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have revolutionized medical image analysis,
they are traditionally limited to specific tasks like classification or segmentation. They
answer ”What is this?” but struggle to answer ”Why?. Vision-Language Models
(VLMs) bridge this gap by combining Computer Vision (CV) and Natural Language
Processing (NLP), enabling the system to generate descriptive clinical reports and answer
open-ended questions.
2.4.2 Importance and Characteristics in Medicine
Applying generic VLMs to medicine presents unique challenges. General models often
hallucinate or refuse to answer medical queries due to safety filters, behaving like a
”layperson”. Specialized Medical VLMs, such as the one used in our system, address
this through:
6
Open-Ended Conversational Capability: Unlike closed-set classifiers, the model
can engage in multi-turn conversations.
Domain Specificity: It is fine-tuned on biomedical literature (e.g., PubMed Cen-
tral).
Data Efficiency: Utilizing curriculum learning to adapt to medical concepts quickly.

2.5 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
2.5.1 General Overview
Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique designed to address the inherent
limitations of Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically ”Hallucination” and ”Knowl-
edge Cutoff.” Concept: Instead of relying solely on the pre-trained parametric memory
of the LLM (which acts like a closed book), RAG connects the model to an external,
up-to-date knowledge base (open book exam).
The standard RAG process involves three steps:
1.Retrieval: The user’s query is converted into a vector embedding. The system
searches a Vector Database to find document chunks that are semantically similar.
7
2.Augmentation: The retrieved documents are concatenated with the original user
query as ”Context.”
3.Generation: The LLM generates an answer based on both the query and the
provided context.

2.5.2 Advanced Methodology: Agentic RAG
While standard RAG is powerful, it is a linear pipeline. In complex medical scenarios,
this is insufficient. Asclena implements Agentic RAG , which introduces an autonomous
reasoning layer that fuses multi-modal inputs.
Why Agentic?
An ”Agent” is an LLM instance equipped with tools and a feedback loop. It does not
just answer; it reasons .
Multi-Modal Fusion: The agent receives quantitative data (CNN probabilities)
AND qualitative descriptions (VLM text).
Cross-Verification: It checks if the text description matches the numerical prob-
ability (e.g., if VLM says ”Normal” but CNN says ”Pneumonia 90%”, the Agent
flags a conflict).
Evidence Retrieval: It acts as a ”Fact-Checker,” citing specific sources to resolve
ambiguities.
The Verification Workflow in Asclena
The Agentic RAG in our system serves as the Verification Layer . It fuses the visual
probability matrix (from CNNs) with the semantic description (from LLaVA) and vali-
dates them against the stored medical literature.

2.6 How the System Evaluates and Decides
The Agentic RAG component in Asclena is designed to act as a verification and ad-
judication layer rather than a generator that “trusts” model outputs by default. Even
when the Perception model (CNN) and the Interpretation model (VLM) agree, Asclena
enforces a strict policy:
Evidence-Gating Rule: No clinical claim is allowed into the final report
unless it is supported by retrieved medical evidence.
This rule directly targets the failure modes of medical AI systems, especially overcon-
fident narration, hallucination, and unjustified conclusions. Operationally, the Agentic
RAG treats CNN outputs and VLM text as hypotheses that must be validated against
a curated medical knowledge base.
2.6.1 Inputs to the Agent (Claim Interface)
The agent does not directly “see” the raw scan. Instead, it receives a structured bundle
of inputs (a claim interface) produced by the upstream services:
Quantitative Evidence (CNN / Perception): multi-label probabilities p=
[p1, . . . , p K], selected top- kpredictions, decision thresholds, and an explainability
artifact (e.g., Grad-CAM heatmap path).
9
Qualitative Hypotheses (VLM / Interpretation): natural-language findings
and impressions describing what is observed and what it may imply clinically.
Retrieval Tooling (RAG): a semantic search tool over a medical vector database
that returns evidence passages with citations/metadata.
2.6.2 Evaluation Pipeline (Decision Logic)
The evaluation performed by the Agentic RAG can be understood as a deterministic
pipeline with a verification loop:
Step 1: Claim Extraction (From Text to Verifiable Units)
The VLM output is first converted into a set of explicit, verifiable claims:
C={c1, c2, . . . , c n}.
Each claim is represented with attributes such as type (finding vs. diagnosis), polarity
(present vs. absent), and strength (certain vs. uncertain). Examples include:
“Opacity is present” (finding, positive).
“No pleural effusion” (finding, negative).
“Likely pneumonia” (diagnostic hypothesis, uncertain).
This transformation is essential because verification cannot be performed reliably on free-
form text alone.
Step 2: Cross-Model Consistency Check (CNN vs. VLM)
For each claim ci, the agent checks whether it is:
Consistent with CNN outputs (e.g., high probability for the related label),
Contradictory to CNN outputs (e.g., VLM claims “normal” while CNN indicates
strong abnormality),
Unverifiable by CNN (e.g., localization or fine-grained descriptions not covered
by available labels).
Importantly, consistency is never treated as proof . It is only a confidence signal
that influences how conservative the final phrasing should be.
Step 3: Evidence Retrieval (Mandatory Grounding)
For every claim ci(regardless of agreement), the agent generates one or more retrieval
queries to the medical knowledge base. Retrieved passages constitute an evidence set:
E(ci) ={e1, e2, . . . , e m}.
Evidence is evaluated by relevance, source quality (e.g., guidelines and peer-reviewed
reviews preferred), and direct support for the claim. If evidence is insufficient, the agent
revises the query and retries retrieval (verification loop).
10
Step 4: Evidence Sufficiency and Strength Assessment
After retrieval, the agent assigns each claim an evidence status:
Strongly Supported: evidence directly supports the claim.
Weak/Partial Support: evidence is relevant but not decisive (or only supports a
softer phrasing).
Unsupported: evidence is missing or does not support the claim.
Step 5: Final Adjudication (Accept / Accept-with-Uncertainty / Defer)
The agent then decides how (or whether) the claim appears in the final report:
1.Accept (Evidence-Backed): if evidence is strong, the claim is included with
citations.
2.Accept with Uncertainty: if evidence is weak/partial, the claim is included with
conservative language (e.g., “may suggest”, “possible”) and citations.
3.Defer / Reject: if evidence is not available, the claim is excluded or explicitly
deferred as “not supported by retrieved evidence”.
This decision stage enforces the central safety property: no unsupported clinical
statement enters the output .
2.6.3 Comprehensive Outcome Cases
Given the three information sources (CNN, VLM, Evidence), the most important outcome
cases are:
Agreement + Strong Evidence: accept claim with citations and normal confi-
dence.
Agreement + Weak Evidence: accept claim with hedging and explicit uncer-
tainty.
Agreement + No Evidence: defer; do not promote agreement into a clinical
statement.
Contradiction + Strong Evidence (for one side): flag inconsistency; prioritize
evidence-backed phrasing and reduce confidence.
Contradiction + No Evidence: conservative report; explicitly state that the
system cannot verify the claim.
CNN-Only Signal (VLM vague) + Evidence: produce an evidence-grounded
summary guided by quantitative confidence.
VLM-Only Detail (not label-covered) + Evidence: include as an observation
with cautious wording and citations.

2.6.4 Why This Improves Safety and Trust
The primary safety gain arises from transforming model outputs into auditable, evidence-
grounded claims . Instead of presenting the final answer as a monolithic “AI diagnosis”,

for every statement, and (iii) cites sources to justify the report. This shifts the system
from “prediction” to transparent clinical decision support that is more aligned with
real-world medical governance requirements.

3.1 Data Ingestion Layer
The flow begins when the user uploads a medical scan. To ensure low latency and scala-
bility, the system creates a ”Split-Path” for data storage:
Binary Data (The Image): Large files are streamed directly to Object Storage
(MinIO/S3). This keeps the main database lightweight.
Metadata (Context): Patient ID, timestamps, and file paths are stored in Post-
greSQL for relational integrity.
Task Dispatch: The API Gateway pushes a lightweight Task IDtoRabbitMQ ,
decoupling ingestion from processing.
3.2 Parallel Processing Pipeline
Asclena utilizes a ”Fork-Join” pattern to process visual and semantic data simultaneously.
3.2.1 The CV Stream (Perception)
TheComputer Vision Worker fetches the image and runs the CNN model.
Output 1: A probability matrix (e.g., Pneumonia: 0.9).
Output 2: A Class Activation Map (CAM). This heatmap is generated as an image
and saved back to Object Storage.
State Update: The worker writes the heatmap URL and probabilities to Redis
and sets the flag cvready=True .
3.2.2 The VLM Stream (Interpretation)
TheVision-Language Worker runs the LLaVA-Med model.
Output: A natural language description of the scan.
State Update: The worker writes the text findings to Redis and sets the flag
vlmready=True .
3.3 Synchronization and Fusion (The Join)
Since the CV model (fast) and VLM model (slow) finish at different times, Redis acts
as the synchronization barrier. The Agentic RAG process is triggered only when both
cvready andvlmready flags are active. This ensures the Agent has a complete picture
(Quantitative + Qualitative) before verifying facts against the Vector Database .
